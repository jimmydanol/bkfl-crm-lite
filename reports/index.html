Bottom line

One clear job, with fewer distractions.

The CRM is now centered on moving a potential bankruptcy client from first contact to a complete, firm-reviewed file that can be used to begin petition preparation.

Recommendation

Use this as the product-design baseline. Keep improving this exact Intake-to-petition-prep path. Do not add broad post-filing case management until the core workflow is secure, connected, and proven with staff.

Stage 1

Lead

The firm has no Intake answers yet. The next job is to get the person started.

Stage 2

Contact

Some Intake data exists, but answers or Documents still need work.

Stage 3

Matter

Intake data and Document requests are complete. Petition prep can begin.

What changed

A safer Document Review loop.

The redesign focuses on the part of the workflow where the firm must make a real decision: approve, reject, excuse, or continue requesting a Document.

1

Review before follow-up

  • Firm decisions appear before the client chase list.
  • Follow-up stays disabled until every review is finished.
  • An assigned Task connects the message to an owner and due date.
2

Keep the evidence

  • Replacement uploads preserve prior versions.
  • Each decision records the reviewer and date.
  • Activity history shows approvals, rejections, excuses, and reopened work.
3

Communicate clearly

  • Client-facing reasons stay separate from internal notes.
  • Repeat reminders retain the original rejection reason.
  • The firm can edit the message before sending.
Firm judgment stays in control

AI may pre-screen an upload and draft administrative work, but it cannot give final approval. An AI-flag override requires a written firm reason.

Competitive lessons

What BK FastLane learned from Glade and Clio.

The goal was not to copy either product. It was to adopt their strongest operating ideas while keeping Matt's four-part CRM understandable to a solo bankruptcy firm.

Glade

Official site ↗

Glade is the closer comparison because it is designed around bankruptcy work rather than a general sales pipeline.

Best lesson: Intake, Documents, communications, and case work should build one continuous bankruptcy file.
  • Use bankruptcy-specific language and next steps.
  • Let clients answer questions and upload Documents without repeated staff direction.
  • Automate administration while preserving legal judgment for the firm.
Adopted in BK FastLane One Contact record now carries Intake progress, Document progress, review decisions, follow-ups, and Activity toward a specific destination: Petition Prep.

Clio

Official site ↗

Clio is broader, but it demonstrates the operating discipline expected from production legal software.

Best lesson: every piece of work needs an owner, a due date, useful history, and controlled client access.
  • Tie Tasks and communications to the same client record.
  • Keep Document metadata and replacement history.
  • Use stage-based workflows and protected client sharing.
Adopted in BK FastLane Review decisions now have a responsible reviewer; follow-up depends on the assigned Task; upload history stays visible; the demo no longer pretends a static URL is secure.
Production boundary

Strong direction; not ready for real debtor data.

The current site is a synthetic product demonstration. Production readiness requires security, reliable system connections, and server-side accountability—not more screens.

!

Do not put real client information in this demo.

The workflow is suitable for design review and synthetic paralegal testing only.

  1. Attorney, paralegal, and administrator accounts with role-based permissions.
  2. Encrypted data and Document storage, backups, retention rules, and tested recovery.
  3. A live Intake-to-CRM connection with retries, duplicate protection, and visible failures.
  4. Malware scanning, real PDF viewing, file controls, and safe downloads.
  5. An immutable, server-side audit history instead of browser-only history.
  6. Expiring, per-Contact portal invitations and upload links.
  7. Real email and text delivery status, consent controls, and message threading.
  8. Attorney-approved, versioned Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 rules.
  9. Reliable joint-filer ownership for every answer and Document.
  10. End-to-end tests across Intake, storage, communications, review, and export.
Recommended order

Build the dependable path before expanding the product.

1

Complete the real Document contract

Use an append-only upload history with uploader, timestamps, review state, reviewer, decision, client reason, internal note, and replacement relationship.

2

Connect the live Intake

Answers and uploads should update the same Contact automatically. Staff should never retype the debtor's information.

3

Add security and accountability

Implement authentication, permissions, protected storage, expiring access, and an immutable audit trail before real client information enters the CRM.

4

Run a controlled synthetic staff pilot

Test joint filers, unreadable files, missing months, repeat rejections, contradictory answers, and late replacements with real firm staff.

5

Run a small supervised real-client pilot

Keep a manual fallback and verify every export. Expand only after the firm can show dependable handling and recovery from mistakes.

Stay lean

What to measure—and what not to add yet.

Measures that matter

  • Time from Lead creation to first Intake response.
  • Time from Contact creation to petition-prep readiness.
  • Documents waiting on the client versus the firm.
  • Follow-ups and replacement uploads per Contact.
  • Average firm review time after an upload arrives.
  • Files reopened after they appeared complete.

Do not add yet

  • A fifth main navigation area.
  • A complex automation rule builder.
  • Predictive legal recommendations.
  • One-click batch approval of debtor Documents.
  • Broad post-filing case management.
  • A large analytics package.
